A Life Spent Looking

Sudeep Kapoor has spent more than forty-one years behind the lens — long enough to have developed film by hand, mastered studio light, descended into coal mines, and published books. This is his story.

The Beginning

It Started in the Dark

Before Sudeep Kapoor ever stood behind a camera professionally, he stood in a darkroom. His foundation was built in traditional analogue processes — colour negative processing, black-and-white film development, transparencies — where an image was not taken but made, slowly, in trays of chemistry under a red lamp.

The darkroom is a demanding teacher. It teaches that every exposure decision is final, that light is physical, and that a photograph is the end of a long chain of disciplined choices. That discipline became the backbone of everything that followed.

When he moved behind the camera, he carried the darkroom with him: the habit of seeing the finished print before pressing the shutter.

Chaya Kriti

In 1992, Sudeep founded his studio, Chaya Kriti, in South Extension Part II, New Delhi. The name became a base for three decades of work: portrait sessions, model photography, advertising shoots, and the controlled-light craft that separates studio photographers from everyone else.

Studio lighting became a signature. Not the equipment — the understanding. The direction of light, its quality, its fall-off across a face; the ability to shape character out of shadow. These are the skills he now teaches in his lighting masterclasses.

Through a long-standing professional association with Bijan Films, the studio years connected him to production environments, agencies, models, professionals and prominent personalities — including celebrity assignments across advertising and editorial work.

The Field Years

Photographing the India That Builds India

While the studio work continued, another body of work grew in parallel — inside factories, plants and mines.

Sudeep's industrial portfolio spans heavy industry and manufacturing across India — work connected with environments such as Sonalika Tractors, Monnet, Jindal ancillary industries, steel operations, engineering facilities and mining environments.

This is photography without a controlled environment: molten metal, moving machinery, dust, heat, and safety protocols. It demands a photographer who can find the frame inside chaos — and who is willing to go where the frame is.

He descended approximately 300 feet underground into a working coal mine to photograph mining operations. He climbed approximately 300 steps up industrial towers — in full safety gear — to capture elevated factory views, years before drones made such angles routine.

Pan-India assignments took him across the country, building a visual record of Indian industry from the inside.

Editorial Evolution

The Lawyers of India

The editorial chapter of Sudeep's career culminates in a major coffee-table book series documenting distinguished lawyers across India — a project of portraiture at national scale.

Volume I, featuring approximately one hundred lawyers, was published around five years ago. Volume II, documenting more than a hundred more, is nearing completion.

The series demonstrates what four decades of portrait craft make possible: hundreds of individual sittings, each requiring trust, patience and a distinct visual answer — held together as one coherent published work.

Photography Today

Still Working. Now Also Teaching.

Sudeep Kapoor continues to accept commercial, editorial and portrait assignments. But the current era of his career has added a second purpose: passing forward what four decades taught him.

He mentors photographers — beginners finding their footing, working professionals refining their craft, and career-switchers who need honest direction. His teaching draws on something no tutorial can offer: the perspective of a person who worked through every technological disruption photography has faced since the darkroom.
